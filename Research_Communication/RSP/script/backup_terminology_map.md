# Backup — Terminology and Methods of Drought-to-Flood Literature

## When to use
If the panel asks "what does the existing literature look like?", "how fragmented is the field?", or "why is there no standard definition?"

---

### What's plotted

Each marker on this map is one study that looks at both sides, drought followed by flood or the reverse. The colour is the terminology they use, and the shape is the type of variable they measure.

The field splits geographically. The red cluster over China is one research tradition, drought-flood abrupt alternation, almost entirely precipitation-based indices built for monsoon seasonality. Over in the US the blue markers are the "whiplash" literature, and the methods are more mixed, some using precipitation, some using streamflow. Europe has a few green markers, "drought-to-flood transition", and one purple square for circulation-based weather whiplash.

### How they actually detect transitions

The shape shows the variable, but there's also a distinction in how these studies detect events. Some apply a raw threshold directly to the variable, for example flagging any streamflow above the 90th percentile as a flood. Others compute an index first, like SPI or one of the Chinese alternation indices, which standardises or transforms the variable, and then threshold that. Both approaches ultimately threshold something, but they can detect different events, sometimes with opposite trends over the same data. The third approach, used only by Francis et al., classifies atmospheric circulation patterns and defines whiplash as a shift between distinct regimes. No thresholding of a surface variable at all.

### What it means

These two traditions grew up separately. Zhang et al. in 2025 was the first major Chinese review to even reference the Western compound event literature. They use different names for the same phenomenon, different indices, different variables, and the indices calibrated for Chinese monsoon basins don't transfer to maritime climates like ours.

Matanó et al. analysed over eight thousand catchments globally and found that 24 percent of all floods follow or overlap with drought. A quarter of all floods. But every study on this map is looking backwards at what already happened. None of them takes a forecast and asks whether we could have seen the transition coming three to six weeks out.
