# Presentations

## Script conventions
- The script is the primary carrier of meaning. Slides support the script, never duplicate it.
- Write in natural spoken voice. Avoid staccato fragments, em-dash overuse, and AI-sounding rhythm.
- Use flowing sentences that are easy to say aloud, remember under pressure, and deliver at a natural pace.
- Use rhetorical questions as slide transitions to create narrative flow.
- Keep jargon minimal. Define technical terms briefly inline rather than in separate explanations.
- Do not over-explain. The audience is PhD-level.
- Estimate timing at ~140 wpm for natural delivery plus pauses.

## Slide conventions
- Slides are visual-first. Use TikZ diagrams, figures, and short labels rather than bullet lists or sentences.
- Minimal text on slides. If a slide has more than ~15 words of non-label text, it's too much.
- LaTeX Beamer with Madrid theme, 16:9 aspect ratio.
- UCD green (`RGB 0, 102, 51`) as primary colour.
- No navigation symbols. Clean frames.

## Language preferences
- Use "multi-hazard" not "compound hazard" for the research framing.
- Use "atmospheric drivers" not "atmospheric oscillations" or "climate modes."
- Use "explainable AI" not "interpretable ML."
- British spelling: "skilful", "colour", "defence."
- No words like "cascade" or "ripple" for hazard interactions.

## Style
- Be concise. No summaries of what was just done.
- Do not add anchor phrases, labels, or meta-commentary to script files.
- Match terminology between script and slide text exactly.
- When editing the script, preserve the user's spoken voice. Do not rewrite into a different register.
