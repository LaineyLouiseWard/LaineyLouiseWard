## Objective
Convince the audience that predicting compound flood-drought events weeks ahead for Ireland is urgent, unsolved, and exciting.

## Core message
We can forecast temperature and rainfall a few weeks out. But nobody knows whether that skill survives when you ask about something more dangerous: a drought turning into a flood. My research finds out.

---

### Hook (~10s)

Quick show of hands. Who knows what the weather's going to be like in three weeks?

[pause]

Yeah, no one really does. But that time period is where really high-stakes decisions have to be made.

### The farmer (~20s)

[click]

Picture a farmer dealing with a long dry spell. The ground has gone hard, almost waterproof. So when that much-needed rain finally comes, it can't soak in. The flooding is actually worse because of the drought. Ideally, that farmer could have acted in advance of the flood. 

### The predictability desert (~15s)

[click]

This timeframe is known as the subseasonal-to-seasonal. It has a nickname — the predictability desert — because forecast skill is so low here. But here's the thing. We can actually forecast individual variables like temperature and rainfall with some skill at this range. Nobody's tested whether that skill survives when you ask a trickier question: is a drought about to become a flood?

### What I'm doing (~25s)

I'm Lainey, and that's what my PhD is focusing on. I'm building a variable-to-hazard pipeline. Seeing how well the forecast performs for regular variables like temperature and rainfall, down to impact variables at the ground like streamflow, and finally whether we can predict the multi-hazard event itself. And on top of that, I'm asking whether machine learning can help the pipeline pick up patterns that the physics-based models struggle with.

### Close (~10s)

So back to our farmer. Could we warn them three weeks out? Not yet. But I'm working to turn that predictability desert into a window of opportunity for preparation.
