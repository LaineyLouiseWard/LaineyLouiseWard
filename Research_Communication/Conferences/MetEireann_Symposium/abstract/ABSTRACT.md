Title: Evaluating ECMWF subseasonal-to-seasonal forecast skill over Ireland: a first step towards drought-to-flood prediction

Floods and droughts can occur in sequence, with one intensifying the other. A drought hardens the soil and reduces infiltration, so the flood response may be worse for a subsequent rainfall event. Ireland is exposed to both Atlantic storm-driven flooding and periodic drought. Anticipating these sequences weeks to months ahead would support early action for water resource management, agriculture, and disaster preparedness. However, subseasonal-to-seasonal (S2S) prediction (2 weeks to 2 months ahead) has limited skill that varies across variables, regions, and seasons, and no study has assessed S2S forecast skill over Ireland.

The work presented here is the first step towards tracing S2S skill from forecast variables, through impact variables such as streamflow, to the prediction of drought-to-flood events. ECMWF offers two forecast systems that cover S2S timescales, a subseasonal system and a seasonal system, which differ in ensemble size, model physics, and initialisation frequency. This work compares both over Ireland to determine which is more suitable for downstream S2S analysis. The variable-level results establish a baseline from which impact and event-level skill can be assessed, with future work applying hydrological modelling and machine learning along this chain.

Forecasts are verified spatially across Ireland and at 25 Met Éireann stations against both observations and ERA5-Land reanalysis, using deterministic (RMSE, Bias, ACC) and probabilistic (BSS, CRPS) metrics for temperature, precipitation, and wind speed at lead times of 3 to 9 weeks.
