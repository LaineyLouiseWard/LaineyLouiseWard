## Objective
Persuade the reader that we cannot currently answer whether S2S forecasts are skilful for multi-hazard events, and that this research provides the framework to find out.

## Core message
This research develops a verification framework to test whether S2S forecasts can predict multi-hazard events.

---

Decisions about flood and drought risk often depend on forecasts two weeks to two months ahead, the subseasonal-to-seasonal (S2S) timescale. Forecast skill at these lead times is still measured one variable at a time. Yet the most damaging events involve interactions between hazards. When drought depletes soil moisture and subsequent rainfall generates runoff rather than infiltration, the resulting flood reflects a sequence, not an isolated extreme. Whether S2S forecasts carry useful skill for such multi-hazard events, and which atmospheric drivers modulate that skill, remains untested.

This study develops a verification framework for ECMWF SEAS5 seasonal and S2S extended-range ensemble reforecasts over Ireland. Deterministic and probabilistic metrics are used to evaluate forecasts of temperature, precipitation, and wind speed against reanalysis and station observations at five locations across lead-weeks one to nine. This first establishes how single-variable skill decays with lead time. The framework then moves to multi-hazard evaluation, identifying flood–drought transitions in both observations and reforecasts and assessing forecast skill during these events. Explainable AI is applied to determine which atmospheric driver combinations are relevant and where nonlinear relationships exist.

The question this research addresses is: when and where can S2S forecasts predict multi-hazard events, and what atmospheric conditions make that possible?
